Computer and Information Research Scientists


Computer & information research scientists invent and design new technology and find new uses for existing technology. They study and solve complex problems in computing for business, science, medicine, and other uses. Most work for computer systems design and related services firms, scientific research and development companies, or the federal government. Computer and information research scientists held about 26,700 jobs in 2012. The job outlook (2014-24) is 11%. The median pay is $110,620 per year, and $53.18 per hour.

Most computer and information research scientists need a Ph.D. in computer science or a related field, like computer engineering. A Ph.D. usually requires 4-5 years of study after the bachelor's degree, typically in a computer-related field, like computer science or information systems. During the first 2 years in a Ph.D. program, students take a variety of computer science classes. They then choose a specialty and spend the remaining years doing research within that specialty. Computer scientists who work in a specialized field may need knowledge of that field. For example, those working on biomedical applications may have to take some biology classes.

General Qualifications:

  • Analytical skills.
    Must be organized in theit thinking and analyze the results of their research.

  • Communication Skills.
    Must communicate well with programmers and managers and be able to clearly explain their conclusions to people with no technical background.

  • Critical-thinking skills.
    Computer and information scientists work on many comples problems.

  • Detail Orientated.
    Must pay close attention to their work, because a small error can cause a whole project to fail.

  • Logical Thinking.
    Computer algorithms rely on logic. Must have a talent for reasoning.

  • Math skills.
    Must have knowledge of advanced math and other technological topics that're critical in computing.
